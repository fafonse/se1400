Do you ever struggle to get clean lines first try? Like, pencil on paper, you can't get your lines to be steady enough for a final draft? You might think that if it's hard to draw a long line, its better to split it into multiple tiny ones.

DONT DO THAT!!
Why not??

You might be asking, not understanding the harm of the forsaken technique. Today I'll teach you the ways of the line, and how you can move past the horrors of chicken scratching.

What is Chicken Scratching?

Chicken scratching is the intentional (sorta) feathering of lines. Specifically, it's done as a handicap to a lack of skill from the artist. If you're ever wondering if it's chicken scratching or style, just ask yourself if you could do the line in a single pass without feathering it. If you can't, then it's chicken scratching.

It's important to differentiate chicken scratching from style. A lot of artists will claim chicken scratching as part of their style, even if it's born from novice art skills. You got to know the rules before you can break them, and style is all about breaking the rules. If you have to rely on chicken scratching to produce the lines you want, then it isn't a stylistic choice, but rather a crutch.

Why Stop?

Some people may argue that chicken scratching isn't all that bad.


They're wrong.


First off, chicken scratching in general looks worse compared to normal lines. You lose a lot of control over the line weight and direction, as the inherent fuzziness hides the line intent. Sharp curves look smoother, and line weight must be less subtle to compensate. If you're feathering a line with a 1px brush, that line has to be at least 3 or 4 pixels wide with the added feathering. Even if you "trim" off the excess, you lose a consistent line weight, as you can't precisely erase the same amount on both sides of the line.

However, the bigger issue is that chicken scratching prevents further artistic development. While continual growth isn't a goal for everyone, chicken scratching is a relatively early hurdle in artistic development. Even if only used for sketching, chicken scratching obscures details and makes it harder for the artist to later retrace a polished final line art. Chicken scratching prevents the artist from learning line confidence, which is essential to good sketches and line art. Line confidence is how you can get clean lines, steady line weights, and real dynamism in the drawing.

Beyond the Scratch

Well, how do you fix chicken scratching?

Line confidence isn't an easy skill to pick up overnight, and redrawing lines over and over again can really slow down drawing speed in the beginning. It can also be scary losing what many feel is part of their personal style.

If you're still hung up on chick scratching as part of your personal style, try figuring out what it adds to your drawings. What role does chicken scratching fill? In good art, everything is intentional (even the random stuff). So you got to ask yourself, what is chicken scratching doing for you?

If you want that increased line "energy", good line confidence can help you add direction to the energy, supporting the line with more direction and intention. Want that dynamic line weight? Learn to control your pen pressure and stability, and you can draw gorgeous thick lines that are amazingly smooth. Love that sketchy quality? Draw overlapping lines instead, which can slowly build up a shape and add thickness without any fuzziness.

When it comes to good exercises for practicing your line art, I'd say one of the most underrated and easiest to do is to simply draw with pen. Pencil gives you tons of leeway with mistakes, but with line art you really want to get it right within a couple strokes. Ink forces you to make each mark with deliberation, forcing you to know what you want to draw before you put ink down. It's going to look rough for a bit, but after a while, you can really visualize what you want without construction. Of course, use reference as much as you'd like, but it's more important that you gain line confidence than be "correct".
